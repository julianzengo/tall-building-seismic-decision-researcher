# Source Hierarchy

This file defines how the researcher treats different sources. Source credibility is not uniform. The researcher must name the tier of every source cited and must not treat lower-tier sources as equivalent to higher-tier sources.

---

## Tier 1 — Authoritative (Code and Regulatory)

These sources define minimum legal and professional requirements within their jurisdiction. They are the baseline against which all other guidance is measured.

**EN 1998 — Eurocode 8: Design of Structures for Earthquake Resistance**
- Part 1: General rules, seismic actions, rules for buildings
- Part 2: Bridges
- Part 3: Assessment and retrofitting of buildings
- Part 5: Foundations, retaining structures, geotechnical aspects

**National Annexes to EN 1998**
National Annexes set Nationally Determined Parameters (NDPs) including seismic zone maps, importance factors, ground type definitions, and permitted deviations from the base standard. The applicable National Annex must always be identified. Designs based on EN 1998 without specifying the NA are incomplete.

**How to use Tier 1:**
- Cite the specific EN part, section, and clause
- Name the National Annex in force for the project jurisdiction
- Flag where the NA modifies the base EN requirement significantly
- Flag where the building exceeds the scope of the prescriptive code (height limits, irregularity thresholds) and requires supplementary judgment

## Tier 2 — Research-Grade

These sources represent the strongest available research evidence outside of code requirements. They are produced by institutions with rigorous peer review and broad expert input. For tall seismic buildings that exceed standard code scope, Tier 2 sources are frequently the primary technical basis for engineering decisions.

**PEER — Pacific Earthquake Engineering Research Center**
University of California Berkeley. Produces research reports on ground motion characterization, structural performance, performance-based earthquake engineering (PBEE) methodology, soil-structure interaction, and tall building behavior. Reports are numbered (e.g., PEER 2010/05). Always cite by report number and year.

Key PEER resources for tall buildings:
- Tall Buildings Initiative (TBI) Guidelines for Performance-Based Seismic Design of Tall Buildings
- PEER Ground Motion Database and NGA-West2 ground motion models
- Reports on core wall behavior, coupling beam performance, and nonlinear analysis

**fib — International Federation for Structural Concrete**
Produces Bulletins covering concrete design, detailing, assessment, and seismic behavior. fib Bulletins are internationally recognized and heavily referenced in EC8-aligned design environments.

Key fib resources:
- fib Bulletin 39: Seismic bridge design and retrofit
- fib Bulletin 68: Punching of flat slabs under combined gravity and drift loading
- fib Model Code 2010/2020: Reference for concrete behavior models

**NIST — National Institute of Standards and Technology**
Produces GCR reports on seismic design, nonlinear analysis procedures, and structural performance. Particularly relevant for analysis methodology.

Key NIST resources:
- NIST GCR 10-917-5: Nonlinear structural analysis for seismic design
- NIST GCR 11-917-10: Selecting and scaling earthquake ground motions
- NIST GCR 17-917-46: Guidelines for nonlinear structural analysis of buildings

**How to use Tier 2:**
- Cite by publication title, institution, report number, and year
- Note whether findings are based on experimental testing, analytical studies, or both
- Flag if findings are validated across multiple studies or represent a single research effort
- Flag if there is ongoing scientific debate on the topic

## Tier 3 — Professional Guidance

These sources represent consensus professional practice guidance, typically produced by engineering associations or councils. They carry strong credibility and are widely used in professional practice, but they are guidance documents rather than code — they do not carry the same legal authority as Tier 1 sources.

**LATBSDC — Los Angeles Tall Buildings Structural Design Council**
An Alternative Procedure for Seismic Design and Analysis of Tall Buildings. Widely used for performance-based design of tall buildings in high-seismicity regions. Provides detailed guidance on ground motion selection, nonlinear modeling, and acceptance criteria.

**CTBUH — Council on Tall Buildings and Urban Habitat**
Guidance on tall building structural systems, height definitions, and global case studies. Useful for precedent research and system selection context.

**SEAOC — Structural Engineers Association of California**
SEAOC Bluebook: Seismic Design Recommendations. Provides commentary and guidance that supplements code requirements, particularly for complex structural systems.

**ACI — American Concrete Institute**
- ACI 318: Building Code Requirements for Structural Concrete — primary US concrete design code, frequently referenced alongside EC8 for detailing guidance where EC8 is less prescriptive
- ACI 369: Guide for Seismic Rehabilitation of Existing Concrete Frame Buildings

**How to use Tier 3:**
- Cite the document, edition, and section
- Note that these are guidance documents, not code — compliance is professional judgment, not legal requirement in EC8 jurisdictions
- Note where Tier 3 guidance supplements EC8 on topics the code addresses less specifically (e.g., nonlinear analysis procedure detail, tall building ground motion selection)

---

## Tier 4 — Academic Research

Peer-reviewed journal articles and conference proceedings with full peer review. Strong credibility for specific technical findings, but must be assessed for scope, validation, and whether findings are widely accepted or contested.

**Key journals:**
- Earthquake Engineering and Structural Dynamics (EESD)
- Earthquake Spectra
- Engineering Structures
- Journal of Structural Engineering (ASCE)
- Bulletin of Earthquake Engineering
- Journal of Earthquake Engineering

**How to use Tier 4:**
- Cite author, journal, year, DOI
- Flag whether findings are supported by experimental testing, analytical work, or both
- Flag if findings are contested or have been contradicted by subsequent work
- Do not treat a single journal article as settled evidence for a major design decision

---

## Tier 5 — Secondary Sources

Design guides from software vendors, manufacturer literature, conference papers without rigorous peer review, general web content, and non-peer-reviewed blog or technical posts.

**How to use Tier 5:**
- Useful for orientation and scoping — not for evidential support of engineering decisions
- Always verify any specific technical claim against Tier 1–3 before using in a conclusion
- Flag the source tier explicitly when citing

---

## When Sources Conflict

Source conflicts must be named, not resolved silently. The format:

1. State the conflict: Source A (Tier X) states [claim]. Source B (Tier Y) states [different claim].
2. Assess why they differ: different jurisdictions, different research scope, different date, methodological difference?
3. State which source is more applicable to this project context and why
4. Flag if the conflict represents unresolved scientific or professional debate
5. Recommend what additional investigation would help resolve the conflict for this project
